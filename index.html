Understanding Fertility Decline:
Exploring State-Level Variations
and Influencing Factors in the US

Over the past fifty years, fertility rates in the US have plummeted and are currently at a historic low.

Conversations about why fertility has fallen so substantially and how we can address the implications of this shift for government programs like social security have been quite salient in recent public discourse and in the 2024 election cycle.

Interestingly, there is significant variation in fertility rates across US states. We’d like to understand the relative importance of various factors in determining a state’s fertility rate.

We used our previous knowledge and assumptions to create a 15 dataset of predictors that we believe may influence fertility rates. Because fertility rate is evaluated statewide and states vary significantly in population size, we have decided that for all of the predictors, we are going to essentially normalize them by looking at the percentage of each state that fall into a specific category.

2022 Fertility Rates for States in the US

*General fertility rate per 1,000 women aged 15–44
you can drag around the bubble chart to see the fertility rate for each state

South Dakota has the highest fertility rate, followed by Alaska then Nebraska.

The general fertility rate in 2022 averaged 56.1 births per 1,000 women aged 15–44 across the United States. Notably, the South and Midwest regions led the country with the highest fertility rates, reflecting trends influenced by factors like regional demographics, cultural norms, and socioeconomic conditions.

Relationship between Fertility Rate and Variables

The third visualization is a scatter plot graph that shows the relationship between the response and the predictor variables involved with this project.

This visualization explores the relationship between fertility rates and poverty measures across U.S. regions, highlighting distinct patterns.

Top Variables impacting Fertility Rate for each Respective Region

The South stands out with higher values for political conservatism, poverty, and restrictive abortion policies, reflecting strong socioeconomic and cultural drivers.

The Northeast emphasizes progressive policies, lower poverty, and less religious influence, while the Midwest shows a balanced mix of factors without a dominant driver.

The West highlights the influence of immigration and racial diversity, with higher proportions of foreign-born and Hispanic populations shaping fertility patterns.
